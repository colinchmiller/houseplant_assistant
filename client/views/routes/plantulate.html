NASA

In 1989, NASA conducted and published a study on the ability of common houseplants to remove harmful toxins from the air. The chemicals that NASA focused on were:

Benzene

Molecule

Benzene is a natural constituent of crude oil and is one of the most common industrial chemicals in use, usually used as an intermediate to make other chemicals, most of which are used mainly in plastics and synthetic fibers. As a result, it it present at low levels in a wide array of products. Benzene is considered a carcinogen to both humans and animals, targeting the liver, kidneys, heart, brain, and bone marrow. It can cause DNA and chromosomial damage and can cause leukemia in sufficient levels. OSHA recommends a maximum exposure limit of 0.1 ppm over 8 hours.

Trichloroethylene

Molecule

Trichloroethylene is typically used as an industrial solvent and in refrigerant manufacture, and was historically used as an anesthetic. It affects primarily the central nervous system, although it can also affect the liver and kidneys, causing intoxication similar to that of alcohol. SCOEL lists the maximum exposure limit of 10 ppm over 8 hours, or 30 ppm for a short term limit (15 minutes).

Formaldehyde

Molecule

Formaldehyde is a naturally occurring organic compound that is commonly used as a precurosr in industrial resins and coatings. It is also created by forest fires, automobile exhaust, and tobacco smoke, thus occurring wherever there is smog. It is toxic to all animals, causing eye and nasal irritation and is a known carcinogen. The maximum recommended concentration level is listed at 0.3 ppm.

Houseplants

There are several criteria that make for a good houseplant. The interior of a person's home, particularly in homes in areas with a distinct winter season, can be difficult for a houseplant to survive in.

Light

Many plants have difficulty indoors due to low lighting. As a result, many indoor plants are tropical understory plants, which are used to lower light levels.

Space Needs

Plants need to be able to survive in small spaces indoors to survive. This includes limited pot space, which restricts root growth and can lead to nutrient depletion.

Water

Water in both the soil and the air can both affect the ability for a plant to survive. The more reilient a plant is in variations of water abundance, the more likely it is to survive indoors.

Toxicity

Many household plants are toxic to varying degrees of severity.
