NASA

NASA

NASA understood that plants could clean the air, and they were interested in seeing if small houseplants, which were already shown to survive in containers, could be used as a means of cleaning the air in space stations, rather than expensive air filtering equipment. More specifically, they wanted to see if the plants could remove common chemicals in the air that could be harmful to human health. These chemicals were:

Benzene

Benzene is a natural constituent of crude oil and is one of the most common industrial chemicals in use, usually used as an intermediate to make other chemicals, most of which are used mainly in plastics and synthetic fibers. As a result, it it present at low levels in a wide array of products. Benzene is considered a carcinogen to both humans and animals, targeting the liver, kidneys, heart, brain, and bone marrow. It can cause DNA and chromosomial damage and can cause leukemia in sufficient levels. OSHA recommends a maximum exposure limit of 0.1 ppm over 8 hours.

Trichloroethylene

Trichloroethylene is typically used as an industrial solvent and in refrigerant manufacture, and was historically used as an anesthetic. It affects primarily the central nervous system, although it can also affect the liver and kidneys, causing intoxication similar to that of alcohol. SCOEL lists the maximum exposure limit of 10 ppm over 8 hours, or 30 ppm for a short term limit (15 minutes).

Formaldehyde

Formaldehyde is a naturally occurring organic compound that is commonly used as a precurosr in industrial resins and coatings. It is also created by forest fires, automobile exhaust, and tobacco smoke, thus occurring wherever there is smog. It is toxic to all animals, causing eye and nasal irritation and is a known carcinogen. The maximum recommended concentration level is listed at 0.3 ppm.

NASA sealed a variety of plants in containers with these chemicals present and measured the amount that was absorbed by the plants. They also did this with the plants trimmed away, and discovered that it is not only the plant that removes the chemicals from the air, but the bacteria in the soil that the plants’ root systems cultivated. But why did NASA decide to use houseplants and not other plants? Well, for space constraints, of course. But houseplants also tend to have a better survival rate for indoor conditions, which can be hostile to plants. Let’s take a look at why this is.

Houseplants Overview

Houseplants

The interior of homes is a difficult place for plants to survive. This is no real surprise: homes are designed to be shelters for people from the elements. This causes three factors to limit a plant’s ability to survive. These are: lack of light, lack of water, and lack of soil/ soil nutrients in which to grow. Because of these limits, plant grown indoors generally have to be tough or require a great deal of effort to maintain. Let’s take a look at each of these limits on plants.

Light

Light

Light is one of the most important things for a plant to have, and is also one of the factors that can vary the most inside a person’s home. Depending on the room’s number of windows, which way their facing, and whether there are any outside trees or buildings that could block sunlight getting in can result in full, bright, direct sunlight or almost non-existent light. In most homes, however, light levels tend to skew to the lower side. As a result, many popular houseplants do not require as much light to survive. These are often tropical understory plants that are used to low light conditions.

To determine how bright your room is for picking the right houseplant, keep in mind that northern windows receive the least light, while southern-facing windows receive the most. Space Requirements

Space Needs

Indoor plants obviously have less space available to them than if they were outdoors. The shortage is both in container space, which limits the ability of roots to spread out and find enough nutrients and water to feed the plant, and in the air height and width, which restricts the ability for the plant to spread out branches or leaves to collect enough sunlight to grow. Because windows, the light source for plants, are usually on the walls and not in the ceilings, plants have less incentive to grow tall indoors.

WATER

Water

Plants need water to process light energy into food. Generally, the less light that a plant is exposed to, the less water it needs to survive. But that’s not the only part of watering plants. Each plant variety requires different levels of water to survive, based upon the environment in which they’ve evolved. For some, having the roots frequently saturated with water will rot them, others depend on it to thrive. Every plant is more or less flexible with the water levels it needs. Many houseplants can handle infrequent watering periods, but not all.

In choosing the best houseplant for you, we will use frequency of watering as an indicator of the plants’ health. This doesn’t mean that a specific plant needs more or less water overall, but more how tough it is in the face of neglect.
